1. Introduction to Motion

Motion is one of the most fundamental concepts of Physics. A body is said to be in motion if its position changes with time with respect to a fixed reference point. If the position of the body does not change with time, the body is said to be at rest.

Examples of Motion
  • 🚗 A moving car on a road.
  • ✈ An aeroplane flying in the sky.
  • 🚆 A train moving on railway tracks.
  • 🌍 Earth revolving around the Sun.
  • 🛰 Satellite orbiting the Earth.
🎯 NDA Tip Always remember— Motion depends upon the Reference Point. The same object may appear at rest to one observer and in motion to another.

2. Reference Point (Origin)

To describe the position of an object, we choose a fixed point called the Reference Point (Origin). All positions are measured with respect to this point.

Reference Point (Origin) ↓ Measure Position ↓ Compare Initial & Final Position ↓ Object at Rest or in Motion
Position = Distance from Reference Point

3. Distance and Displacement

Whenever an object moves from one place to another, we can describe its motion using two quantities: Distance and Displacement. Although both measure motion, they are different physical quantities.

Distance Displacement
Total length of the actual path travelled. Shortest straight-line distance between initial and final position.
Scalar Quantity Vector Quantity
Always Positive May be Positive, Negative or Zero
Depends on actual path. Depends only on initial and final positions.
Distance ≥ Displacement Displacement ≤ Distance
SI Unit = metre (m) SI Unit = metre (m)
Distance = Length of Actual Path

Displacement = Shortest Distance between Initial & Final Position
Example

Suppose a student walks 4 m East and then 3 m West.

Distance = 4 + 3 = 7 m
Displacement = 1 m East
Start ● │ │ 4 m East ↓ ●────────────● │ │ 3 m West ↑ Final Position
🎯 NDA Shortcut
  • Distance is always greater than or equal to displacement.
  • If an object returns to its starting point, displacement becomes 0, but distance is never zero.

4. Scalar and Vector Quantities

Physical quantities are divided into two categories.

Physical Quantities │ ├── Scalar Quantities │ (Magnitude only) │ └── Vector Quantities (Magnitude + Direction)
Scalar Quantity Vector Quantity
Only Magnitude Magnitude + Direction
Cannot specify direction. Direction is compulsory.
Simple Addition Vector Addition Rules
Speed Velocity
Distance Displacement
Mass Force
Time Acceleration
Easy Trick

Scalar → Size only
Vector → Size + Direction

Solved Example 1

Question: A person walks 10 m towards East and then returns 10 m towards West. Find

  1. Distance
  2. Displacement

Distance = 10 + 10 = 20 m

Displacement = 0 m (Because Initial Position = Final Position)
Important Point Whenever an object completes one full round and returns to its starting point, Distance ≠ 0 but Displacement = 0.

5. Speed and Velocity

The terms Speed and Velocity are often used interchangeably, but in Physics they have different meanings. Speed tells us how fast an object is moving, whereas velocity tells us how fast and in which direction the object is moving.

Speed Velocity
Scalar Quantity Vector Quantity
Distance / Time Displacement / Time
No Direction Direction Required
Always Positive May be Positive, Negative or Zero
Depends on Distance Depends on Displacement
Speed = Distance ÷ Time

Average Speed = Total Distance ÷ Total Time

Velocity = Displacement ÷ Time

Average Velocity = Total Displacement ÷ Total Time
SI Unit
  • Speed → metre/second (m/s)
  • Velocity → metre/second (m/s)
🎯 NDA Tip If an object returns to its starting point, Average Velocity = 0 because displacement becomes zero.

Solved Example 2

Question: A car travels 120 km in 2 hours. Find its average speed.

Average Speed = Distance ÷ Time = 120 ÷ 2 = 60 km/h

6. Uniform and Non-Uniform Motion

Depending upon how an object moves, motion is classified into two types.

Motion │ ├── Uniform Motion │ Equal Distance │ Equal Time │ └── Non-Uniform Motion Unequal Distance Equal Time
Uniform Motion Non-Uniform Motion
Equal distances in equal intervals of time. Unequal distances in equal intervals of time.
Speed remains constant. Speed changes continuously.
Acceleration = Zero Acceleration ≠ Zero
Straight Line Distance-Time Graph Curved Distance-Time Graph
Example: Train moving at constant speed. Example: Car moving in city traffic.
Examples
  • 🚆 Express train moving at constant speed → Uniform Motion
  • 🚗 Car moving in heavy traffic → Non-Uniform Motion
  • 🏃 Runner changing speed → Non-Uniform Motion
  • ✈ Aircraft flying steadily → Uniform Motion

Solved Example 3

Question: A bus covers 50 km in the first hour, 60 km in the second hour and 70 km in the third hour. Is the motion uniform?

Distances covered 50 km 60 km 70 km They are not equal. Therefore, Motion is Non-Uniform.
💡 Remember Uniform Motion ✔ Constant Speed ✔ Zero Acceleration

Non-Uniform Motion ✔ Changing Speed ✔ Acceleration Present

7. Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. Whenever the velocity of an object changes (either magnitude or direction), the object is said to be accelerating.

Acceleration (a) = (Final Velocity − Initial Velocity) ÷ Time

a = (v − u) / t
Quantity Symbol SI Unit
Initial Velocity u m/s
Final Velocity v m/s
Acceleration a m/s²
Time t second (s)
Change in Velocity ↓ Acceleration ↓ Positive (Object speeds up) OR Negative (Object slows down)
Types of Acceleration
  • ✔ Positive Acceleration – Velocity increases.
  • ✔ Negative Acceleration (Retardation) – Velocity decreases.
  • ✔ Zero Acceleration – Velocity remains constant.
  • ✔ Uniform Acceleration – Equal change in velocity in equal intervals of time.
🎯 NDA Tip An object moving with constant speed in a circular path is still accelerated because its direction changes continuously.

Solved Example 4

Question: A car increases its velocity from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 5 seconds. Find the acceleration.

Given u = 10 m/s v = 30 m/s t = 5 s

a = (30 − 10)/5

a = 20/5

Acceleration = 4 m/s²

8. Distance-Time Graph

A Distance-Time Graph shows how the distance travelled by an object changes with time. The Slope of the graph represents Speed.

Distance (m) | | / | / | / | / |/ +---------------------- Time (s)
Graph Meaning
Straight Line Uniform Speed
Curved Line Non-Uniform Speed
Horizontal Line Object at Rest
Slope of Distance-Time Graph = Speed

9. Velocity-Time Graph

A Velocity-Time Graph represents the change in velocity with time. The slope gives Acceleration and the area under the graph gives Displacement.

Velocity (m/s) | | / | / | / | / |__/ +---------------------- Time (s)
Property Meaning
Slope Acceleration
Area under Graph Displacement
Horizontal Line Constant Velocity
Positive Slope Positive Acceleration
Negative Slope Retardation
💡 Quick Revision
  • Distance-Time Graph → Slope = Speed
  • Velocity-Time Graph → Slope = Acceleration
  • Area under Velocity-Time Graph = Displacement

10. Equations of Motion

The following equations are applicable only for uniformly accelerated rectilinear motion. These are the most important formulas for NDA, JEE and Board examinations.

Symbol Meaning
u Initial Velocity
v Final Velocity
a Acceleration
t Time
s Displacement
First Equation of Motion

v = u + at
Second Equation of Motion

s = ut + ½at²
Third Equation of Motion

v² = u² + 2as
🎯 NDA Tip Always remember the sequence: Velocity → Displacement → Velocity² This helps in recalling all three equations easily.

Solved Example 5

Question: A body starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s² for 5 seconds. Find its final velocity.

Given u = 0 a = 2 m/s² t = 5 s

v = u + at
v = 0 + (2 × 5)
v = 10 m/s

Solved Example 6

Question: A car moves with an initial velocity of 10 m/s and accelerates at 3 m/s² for 4 seconds. Find the displacement.

s = ut + ½at²

= (10 × 4) + ½ × 3 × 4²

= 40 + 24

s = 64 m

11. Uniform Circular Motion

When an object moves along a circular path with constant speed, its motion is called Uniform Circular Motion. Although the speed remains constant, the direction changes continuously. Therefore, the velocity changes continuously. Hence, the object is always accelerated.

Constant Speed ↓ Direction Changes ↓ Velocity Changes ↓ Acceleration Exists
Property Uniform Circular Motion
Speed Constant
Velocity Changes Continuously
Acceleration Always Present
Direction Changes at Every Point
Examples
  • 🌍 Earth revolving around the Sun
  • 🛰 Artificial satellite around Earth
  • 🪨 Stone tied to a string and rotated
  • 🌀 Ceiling fan blades
  • 🎡 Giant wheel

📌 NDA Formula Sheet

Speed = Distance / Time

Velocity = Displacement / Time

Acceleration = (v − u)/t

v = u + at

s = ut + ½at²

v² = u² + 2as
