1. What is Matter?

Everything around us that has mass and occupies space is called Matter. Everything that we can see, touch, smell or feel is made up of matter.

Examples of Matter
  • 💧 Water
  • 🌳 Wood
  • 🪨 Stone
  • 🌬 Air
  • 📖 Book
  • 🧑 Human Body
🎯 NDA Tip Matter always has:
  • ✔ Mass
  • ✔ Volume (Occupies Space)
Anything lacking these two properties is not considered matter.

2. Physical Nature of Matter

Matter is made up of extremely tiny particles called particles of matter. These particles are so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye.

Matter ↓ Tiny Particles ↓ Atoms ↓ Molecules
Activity Add a spoon of sugar into water. The sugar disappears, but the water tastes sweet. This proves that matter is made of tiny particles.

3. Characteristics of Particles of Matter

The tiny particles of matter possess certain important characteristics. These properties explain the behaviour of solids, liquids and gases.

Particles of Matter │ ├── Have Spaces Between Them ├── Are Continuously Moving ├── Attract Each Other └── Are Extremely Small

3.1 Particles have Spaces Between Them

There are empty spaces between the particles of matter. These spaces are called inter-particle spaces. The amount of space varies in solids, liquids and gases.

Activity Take 100 mL of water in a measuring cylinder. Add a spoon of sugar and stir. The sugar disappears but the water level changes very little. This proves that particles of matter have spaces between them.

3.2 Particles are Continuously Moving

Particles of matter are always in motion because they possess kinetic energy. As temperature increases, the kinetic energy also increases.

Higher Temperature ↓ Higher Kinetic Energy ↓ Faster Movement of Particles
Example The smell of perfume spreads throughout a room because its particles move continuously.

3.3 Particles Attract Each Other

Particles of matter attract one another. This attractive force keeps the particles together. The strength of attraction is different for different states of matter.

State Force of Attraction
Solid Very Strong
Liquid Moderate
Gas Very Weak
🎯 NDA Tip Greater the force of attraction, harder it is to separate the particles.

3.4 Particles are Extremely Small

Matter is made up of extremely tiny particles which cannot be seen by naked eyes. Millions of particles can exist in a very small amount of matter.

Example One crystal of potassium permanganate can colour a large quantity of water because it contains millions of tiny particles.

4. States of Matter

On the basis of the arrangement and behaviour of particles, matter is mainly classified into five states.

Matter │ ├── Solid ├── Liquid ├── Gas ├── Plasma └── Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC)
State Shape Volume Particle Arrangement Example
Solid Fixed Fixed Closely Packed Ice, Stone
Liquid No Fixed Shape Fixed Less Closely Packed Water, Milk
Gas No Fixed Shape No Fixed Volume Far Apart Air, Oxygen
Plasma No Fixed Shape No Fixed Volume Ionised Particles Sun, Lightning
Bose–Einstein Condensate Quantum State Very Small Atoms Behave as One Ultra-cold Rubidium Atoms

5. Plasma

Plasma is known as the fourth state of matter. It is formed when a gas is heated to a very high temperature. The atoms lose electrons and become ionised. Plasma contains positive ions and free electrons.

Examples
  • ☀ Sun
  • ⭐ Stars
  • ⚡ Lightning
  • 💡 Neon Sign Boards
  • 🔦 Fluorescent Lamps

6. Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC)

Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC) is called the fifth state of matter. It is formed when a gas is cooled to a temperature very close to absolute zero (0 K or −273.15°C). At this temperature, atoms lose their individual identity and behave like a single quantum particle.

Important Facts
  • Predicted by Satyendra Nath Bose and Albert Einstein.
  • First created experimentally in 1995.
  • Used in modern quantum physics research.
🎯 NDA Fact Remember the order of states of matter: Solid → Liquid → Gas → Plasma → Bose–Einstein Condensate (BEC)

7. Properties of Solids

A solid is a state of matter in which the particles are very closely packed due to strong intermolecular forces.

Property Description
Shape Definite Shape
Volume Definite Volume
Particle Arrangement Closely Packed
Force of Attraction Very Strong
Compressibility Negligible
Diffusion Very Slow
Examples
  • 🧊 Ice
  • 🪨 Stone
  • 📚 Book
  • 🪑 Chair
  • 🪙 Coin
Particles ⬤⬤⬤⬤⬤ ⬤⬤⬤⬤⬤ ⬤⬤⬤⬤⬤ Very Closely Packed

8. Properties of Liquids

A liquid has a fixed volume but no fixed shape. It takes the shape of the container in which it is kept.

Property Description
Shape No Fixed Shape
Volume Fixed
Particle Arrangement Less Closely Packed
Force of Attraction Moderate
Compressibility Very Low
Diffusion Moderate
Examples
  • 💧 Water
  • 🥛 Milk
  • 🛢 Oil
  • 🧃 Juice
⬤ ⬤⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤⬤ Particles can slide over one another.

9. Properties of Gases

A gas has neither definite shape nor definite volume. Its particles move freely in all directions.

Property Description
Shape No Fixed Shape
Volume No Fixed Volume
Particle Arrangement Very Far Apart
Force of Attraction Very Weak
Compressibility Very High
Diffusion Very Fast
Examples
  • 🌬 Air
  • 🫧 Oxygen
  • 🔥 Hydrogen
  • 💨 Carbon Dioxide
⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ Particles are very far apart and move freely.
🎯 NDA Quick Comparison
Property Solid Liquid Gas
Shape Fixed No No
Volume Fixed Fixed No
Compressibility Very Low Low Very High

Particle Motion Vibrate Slide Move Freely

Solved Example 1

Question: Why are gases more compressible than solids?

Solution: Gas particles have very large intermolecular spaces. When pressure is applied, these spaces decrease easily. Hence gases are highly compressible, whereas solids are almost incompressible because their particles are tightly packed.
💡 Memory Trick Solid → Strongest Force
Liquid → Medium Force
Gas → Weakest Force This order is frequently asked in NDA Foundation exams.

10. Diffusion

Diffusion is the spontaneous movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration until they are uniformly distributed. Diffusion occurs in all three states of matter, but its speed differs.

High Concentration ↓ Particles Move Randomly ↓ Low Concentration ↓ Uniform Distribution
State of Matter Rate of Diffusion Reason
Solid Very Slow Particles are tightly packed.
Liquid Moderate Particles have some free space.
Gas Very Fast Particles are far apart and move freely.
Examples of Diffusion
  • 🌸 Perfume smell spreads throughout a room.
  • 🍵 Tea leaves spread colour in hot water.
  • 🧂 Salt dissolves uniformly in water.
  • 💨 Incense stick fragrance spreads in air.
🎯 NDA Tip Diffusion becomes faster when temperature increases because particles gain more kinetic energy.

11. Change of State of Matter

Matter can change from one state to another by changing temperature or pressure.

Solid ↓ (Melting) ↓ Liquid ↓ (Boiling) ↓ Gas ↓ (Condensation) ↓ Liquid ↓ (Freezing) ↓ Solid

12. Effect of Temperature

When heat is supplied, particles gain kinetic energy and move faster. This weakens the force of attraction between them and changes their state.

Process State Change Heat
Melting Solid → Liquid Absorbed
Boiling Liquid → Gas Absorbed
Condensation Gas → Liquid Released
Freezing Liquid → Solid Released
Heat ↑ ↓ Particle Energy ↑ ↓ Intermolecular Force ↓ ↓ State Changes

13. Melting Point

The melting point is the temperature at which a solid changes into a liquid under atmospheric pressure.

Example Ice melts at 0°C (273 K).

14. Boiling Point

The boiling point is the temperature at which a liquid changes into vapour throughout its mass.

Example Pure water boils at 100°C (373 K) at normal atmospheric pressure.

15. Effect of Pressure

Increasing pressure brings gas particles closer together. When sufficient pressure is applied and temperature is lowered, gases can be converted into liquids.

Increase Pressure + Decrease Temperature ↓ Gas ↓ Liquid
Examples
  • 🧯 LPG cylinders
  • ⛽ CNG cylinders
  • 🏥 Oxygen cylinders
🎯 NDA Tip Both pressure and temperature determine the state of matter. Increasing pressure generally favours the liquid state.

Solved Example 2

Question: Why does the smell of hot food spread faster than cold food?

Solution: Hot food has a higher temperature. Its particles possess greater kinetic energy and diffuse more rapidly. Hence the smell spreads faster.

16. Latent Heat

When a substance changes its state, it absorbs or releases heat without any change in temperature. This hidden heat is called Latent Heat.

Latent Heat = Heat absorbed or released during change of state without change in temperature.
Type Meaning Example
Latent Heat of Fusion Heat required to convert solid into liquid. Ice → Water
Latent Heat of Vaporisation Heat required to convert liquid into gas. Water → Steam
🎯 NDA Tip During melting and boiling, the temperature remains constant until the complete change of state occurs.

17. Sublimation

Sublimation is the direct conversion of a solid into gas without passing through the liquid state. The reverse process is called Deposition.

Solid ↓ Sublimation ↓ Gas ↓ Deposition ↓ Solid
Examples
  • 🧪 Camphor
  • 🧂 Ammonium Chloride
  • 🧊 Dry Ice (Solid CO₂)
  • 🧼 Naphthalene Balls
  • 🧪 Iodine Crystals

18. Evaporation

Evaporation is the slow conversion of a liquid into vapour from its surface at any temperature below its boiling point.

Evaporation Boiling
Occurs at any temperature. Occurs only at boiling point.
Surface phenomenon. Bulk phenomenon.
Slow process. Fast process.
Liquid ↓ Evaporation ↓ Vapour

19. Factors Affecting Evaporation

Factor Effect on Evaporation
Surface Area More surface area → Faster evaporation.
Temperature Higher temperature → Faster evaporation.
Humidity Lower humidity → Faster evaporation.
Wind Speed Higher wind speed → Faster evaporation.
Increase Temperature ↑ Increase Surface Area ↑ Increase Wind Speed ↑ Decrease Humidity ↓ ↓ Evaporation Increases
Daily Life Examples
  • 👕 Wet clothes dry faster on a sunny day.
  • 🌬 Clothes dry faster under a fan.
  • 🏖 Water evaporates quickly in deserts.
  • 🍵 Hot tea cools because of evaporation.

20. Cooling Effect of Evaporation

During evaporation, high-energy particles escape from the liquid. The remaining particles lose energy, causing the liquid to cool.

Examples
  • 💦 Sweat cools our body.
  • 🏺 Water in an earthen pot remains cool.
  • 💧 Acetone feels cold on the skin.
  • 🌿 Desert coolers work on evaporation.
🎯 NDA Tip Evaporation is responsible for the natural cooling of our body through sweating.
